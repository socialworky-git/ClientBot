What is Motivational Interviewing?

A collaborative, person-centered way of talking with people about change. It draws out a person's own reasons for change rather than imposing ours.

Ambivalence is the starting point

Most people who feel stuck about a change want two things at once. One part argues for staying the same, which we call sustain talk, and another part argues for moving forward, which we call change talk. That pull in both directions is not resistance or denial. It is a normal, human part of facing anything hard.

A way of being

Before any method, MI is a way of being with someone, what its founders call the underlying spirit. Four qualities flow from the helper toward the person: partnership, acceptance, compassion, and empowerment. The skills only do their work when they grow out of this stance.

Evoking change talk

MI runs on a few simple skills, open questions and reflections most of all. A good question or reflection builds momentum, and helps clients hear themselves think aloud and build their own case for change.

The MI roadmap

MI moves through four processes that build on one another: engaging to form the relationship, focusing to find a direction, evoking the person's own motivation, and planning the steps that follow.

One skillset, many settings

MI is a clearly defined skillset, precise enough to be measured, taught, and practiced on purpose. It makes sense in any clinical setting where a client may be ambivalent about some aspect of change, whether that is substance misuse treatment, suicide prevention engagement, basic assessment and diagnostic work, or most behavioral evidence based practice modalities like cognitive behavioral therapy. MI is not a competing model so much as a way of being in the conversation that makes other good work possible.
